By Andrea Perez

The U.S. Latino community is no longer just a demographic trend—it is the engine of the American economy. Now 68 million strong, Latinos represent one-in-five Americans and account for more than half of the nation's total population growth since 2000. The economic impact is staggering: in 2023, the U.S. Latino GDP hit $4 trillion, effectively making it the 5th largest economy in the world if measured as a standalone nation.

While public discourse often narrows its focus to migration, the data reveals a far more significant reality: the Latino community is an indispensable economic titan. By replacing outdated stereotypes with hard data, we see that Latino hard work and entrepreneurship aren’t merely cultural traits—they are the bedrock of modern American prosperity.

This is a story of a community reframed, moving beyond simple demographics to emerge as a primary driver of national wealth. With a workforce of 32 million and a labor participation rate that consistently outpaces the national average, this community has become the nation’s backbone. From 5 million Latino-owned businesses to a record $2.5 trillion in annual spending power, the revelation is clear: the future of the American Dream is being fueled
by the ambition and unstoppable spirit of its Latino population.

Total Latino Population in the U.S.

Representing 20% of the total population, the 68 million Americans
of Latino descent are the nation's largest racial or ethnic minority.

Latinos are among the fastest-growing racial or ethnic groups in the United States. Between 2000 and 2024, the Latino population nearly doubled, rising from 35.3 million to 68 million. Latinos accounted for more than half of all U.S. population growth during that period. Today, Latinos are the country’s second-largest racial or ethnic group, making up one-in-five Americans. They are also strikingly diverse, relatively young, mostly U.S. born and increasingly dispersed across the country.

Latinos Are Major Drivers of the U.S. Economy

The U.S. Latino economy reached $4 trillion in 2023, making it the 5th largest economy
in the world if measured independently.

Latinos are an essential force in America’s economy. The U.S. Latino economy is expected to continue expanding faster than most first-world economies, supported by population growth, youth, business formation, improvements in educational attainment, and general upward mobility.

The Impact of Hispanic-Owned Businesses
on the U.S. Economy

Data from the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics show that there are more than 5 million Latino-owned businesses operating in the country, employing more than 3.5 million workers.

These businesses contribute over $800 billion per year to the U.S. economy through sales trade. Latino businesses with employees are concentrated in key sectors including construction, accommodation & food services, professional services, administrative support, health care and social assistance, and retail.

The Backbone of the U.S. Workforce

The Latino workforce reached an average of 32 million workers in 2025, marking a 5.5% year-over-year increase and a 46.5% rise since 2010.
The Latino labor force participation rate reached 69%, above the national average.

The data highlights a significant shift in the American economic landscape, positioning the Latino community as a primary driver of national labor growth. As older generations exit the workforce, the youthful demographic profile of the Latino community is effectively filling the vacuum, driving expansion in sectors ranging from healthcare to technology. This data highlights a fundamental shift in the American economic landscape, positioning Latino workers not just as participants, but as the primary drivers of national labor growth and the most critical hedge against domestic labor shortages.

Investing in the American Promise

Latino households are a defining force of the U.S. economy.
By 2023, their gross domestic income reached a record $3.1 trillion, up from $1.2 trillion in 2019.

2023 Total Domestic Income (GDI): $3.1 trillion

This surge in earnings enabled the community to contribute an estimated $815 billion in total taxes.
Even after tax obligations, Latino households held over $2.5 trillion in spending power,
driving growth across major American industries.
